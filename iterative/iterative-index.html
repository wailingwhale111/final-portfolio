CSCI 1300: Iterative Design and Evaluation

Background

As a group of computer science students at Brown University, we wanted to iteratively design for a startup that is could be more practical in our daily life. We decided to design for a startup called Taro.

Sketching and Wireframing

To summarize above, Taro is a website designed to be a premium subscription/membership based platform for software engineers who want to further their career. The idea is that with a payment, Taro will give exclusive access to information about jobs and how to get a job ranging from Q&As with leads from top tech companies and creating optimized performance reviews. The idea is to focus on the networking and non-technical side of the job.

After looking at the concept of the startup, we assumed that our users consist of (1) software engineers currently employed, (2) undergraduate students looking for internship or post-grad opportunities, (3) recruiters at different companies. Software engineers who are currently employed would be impacted by this website because then they are able to learn tips and tricks about how to do their job better. Additionally, if they want to pivot companies, this website would provide valuable information for that. Undergraduate students would benefit from this because they are jumping into a field without much prior experience and would benefit from learning how to work as a software engineer and more specifically, how to work as a software engineer for a top tech company. Lastly, recruiters would benefit from the website because they are able to find software engineers who are ambitious and very much either currently on the job hunt or looking to further their career by switching companies. Scenario Summary: You are Jeff Huang, a college computer science professor, who is thinking of going back into the software engineering industry. You bought a subscription to Taro, an exclusive software engineering career resource and networking platform. Your task is to find the Expert Advice article on an Amazon Recruiter's take on the recession and job market. (Note: This is a prototype and not an actual website so interactions may feel quite different).

We analyzed their performance and commentary for each subtask.

Go to sign in and login (Assume that the username and password are prefilled):
  • All users were able to complete the task and all noted that it was an easy process
  • A few users noted how intuitive the location of the sign in button was and that the change to the home page with a profile picture where the sign in button was confirmed that they have successfully signed in
  • This was in line with our expectations as we modeled our sign in process and layout similar to other platforms
Navigate to the Expert Advice page:
  • 2 out of 3 users were able to navigate to the Expert Advice page by clicking on the Expert Advice button on the navigation bar
  • One user directly went to the article from the home screen and was confused by the next subtask. He concluded that the login screen took him to the Expert Advice page as the back button was labeled so
  • Though we expected for some users to directly go to the article, we did not expect his conclusion to be that the home screen to the Expert Advice page due to the back button in the article labeling back to Expert Advice
Go to the article titled "How to handle the recession from an Amazon recruiter's perspective‚Äù:
  • All users were able to go to the article and said that completing the task was very easy
  • This was in line with our expectations as we explicitly stated the article title to look for
Sign out of your account:
  • 2 out of 3 users successfully were able to complete the task and mentioned that the location of the sign out button (in a drop down after clicking profile picture) was very intuitive and followed the structure that all web pages follow
  • One user clicked too far in the subtask step and the figma screen ended, but verbally told us his plan which was correct
  • One user mentioned that a confirmation page that the signout was successful would have been nice
  • This was in line with our expectations as followed the traditional logout location that most web pages follow
Based on the users' feedback, having the back button label be different for the article based on whether they navigated to the article from the Home page or Expert Advice page would help their understanding on what page they previously were in. Additionally, adding a successful sign out page after signing out would help the users confirm that they indeed did sign out. We feel confident in keeping all other aspects the same and the testing results were successful overall.

Conclusion

We learned a lot about the iterative process of making an interaction prototype. For complete transparency, the user test results were from a second set of tests since our first one ended up quite badly due to misassumptions about the testing process and how intuitive the question flow and tasks would be for the users. Thus, we edited the tasks to be much more explicit and learned how much the testing format and wording affect the user's interaction.
